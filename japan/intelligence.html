A: Executive Summary

Japan enters the SSI framework as the 10th country, bringing unique structural challenges: a 50 Hz/60 Hz dual-frequency grid bisected by the Itoigawa-Shizuoka Tectonic Line, connected only through three frequency converter stations (combined 1,200 MW capacity). The initial scoring of 6,003 substations across 47 prefectures reveals a fleet distribution of approximately 15% Low, 46% Medium, 24% High, and 15% Critical — broadly consistent with §9 targets but reflecting Japan’s extreme natural hazard exposure.

The top risk drivers are: (1) Seismic amplification via R6b modifier, with Shizuoka (PGA=0.65g), Mie (0.55g), and Kochi (0.55g) in the Nankai Trough zone receiving 20-40% score amplification; (2) CAIDI-driven isolation penalties for Okinawa (CAIDI=32 min) and Hokkaido (CAIDI=22 min), reflecting island/peninsular grid isolation; (3) Super-ageing society pressure through the Societal (S) component, with 23 of 47 prefectures having elderly ratios above 0.32, increasing both demand vulnerability and workforce constraints.

Key finding: The 50/60 Hz boundary represents a systemic single point of failure. Combined converter station capacity (1,200 MW) represents less than 1% of Japan’s peak demand (~160 GW), making cross-boundary resilience operationally negligible during large-scale events.

E: Deep Dive — Nankai Trough Seismic Zone

The Nankai Trough runs along the Pacific coast from Shizuoka to Miyazaki, posing the highest seismic risk to Japan’s grid infrastructure. Four prefectures in the direct impact zone — Shizuoka (PGA=0.65g), Mie (0.55g), Kochi (0.55g), and Wakayama (0.45g) — receive significant R6b seismic amplification, placing them consistently in the top-10 highest-risk prefectures.

Shizuoka is of particular concern: it sits directly on the projected Tokai earthquake rupture zone and hosts the Hamaoka Nuclear Power Plant corridor. With PGA=0.65g (highest in the fleet), the R6b modifier amplifies Shizuoka substations by up to 34%. The prefecture also spans the 50/60 Hz boundary, making its grid infrastructure a critical node for national resilience. Chubu Electric Power (60 Hz side) and TEPCO (50 Hz side) both serve parts of Shizuoka, creating jurisdictional complexity during emergency response.

Kochi combines high seismic exposure (0.55g) with the lowest population density among Shikoku prefectures (97/km²), the highest elderly ratio in Shikoku (0.362), and severe typhoon exposure (2.0 typhoon days/year average). This compounding of natural hazard, demographic decline, and infrastructure isolation makes Kochi the archetypal high-risk prefecture in the Japanese context.

F: Substation in Focus — Okinawa Grid Isolation

Okinawa Electric Power Company operates Japan’s most isolated grid — a 60 Hz island network with no interconnection to the mainland. With a CAIDI of 32 minutes (nearly 3x the national average of ~12 minutes), Okinawa substations consistently score highest on Continuity (C) components. The isolation means any major outage requires fully local restoration with no import capacity from adjacent EPCO territories.

Compounding factors include: extreme typhoon exposure (3.5 typhoon days/year, highest nationally), coastal atmospheric corrosion (C3 classification), and relatively low digital infrastructure readiness (0.45 MIC index vs 0.78 for Tokyo). However, Okinawa benefits from the lowest seismic risk in Japan (PGA=0.10g, Z-factor=0.7) and the youngest population profile (elderly ratio 0.228, lowest nationally).

The SSI correctly identifies Okinawa as a CAIDI-driven high-risk territory rather than a seismic risk zone — a differentiation that validates the multi-component scoring approach. Risk mitigation priorities for Okinawa focus on typhoon-hardening, BESS deployment for islanded operation, and smart grid modernisation rather than seismic reinforcement.
